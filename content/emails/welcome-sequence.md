## Email 3 — Day 4

**Subject:** The thing in your home that holds the most odor

**Preview text:** It's soft, it's warm, and your dog sleeps on it eight hours a day.

Hi {{ first_name | default: "there" }},

Pet bedding is the single biggest odor reservoir in most homes — and it's usually washed with the strongest fragrance in the laundry aisle. That combination is how you get a bed that smells like lavender over wet dog.

The fix is simpler than the problem: hot water (or the warmest the care tag allows), a fragrance-free detergent, no fabric softener, and a complete dry. Fabric softener is the sneaky one — it coats fibers and traps the very odor you're trying to remove.

We put together the full method, including what to do with non-washable inserts and foam beds: [How to clean dog bedding without harsh fragrances](/pet-home/clean-dog-bedding-without-harsh-fragrances).

## Email 4 — Day 6

**Subject:** Probiotic vs. enzyme cleaners — what's the actual difference?

**Preview text:** Two bottles, two mechanisms, one honest comparison.

Hi {{ first_name | default: "there" }},

The two labels you'll see most in pet-odor cleaning are "enzyme" and "probiotic," and they're genuinely different tools.

Enzyme cleaners use proteins that break down specific organic matter — urine, vomit, feces — and they're very good at fresh accidents. Probiotic cleaners apply beneficial bacteria that keep working on organic soil after application, which suits ongoing maintenance. One is a spot treatment; the other is more of a routine.

Neither is magic, and neither replaces removing the mess first. But choosing the right one for the job saves money and frustration.

We compared them head to head — where each wins, where each disappoints, and which belongs under your sink: [Probiotic cleaner vs. enzyme cleaner](/comparisons/probiotic-cleaner-vs-enzyme-cleaner).

## Email 5 — Day 8

**Subject:** Are probiotic cleaners safe around pets?

**Preview text:** The fair question everyone asks before spraying near the dog bowl.

Hi {{ first_name | default: "there" }},

It's the right question to ask. If you're applying live bacteria to the surfaces your pets lick, sleep on, and eat near, you should want to know what's in the bottle.

The short version: probiotic cleaning products use bacteria selected to be non-pathogenic, and manufacturers formulate them for household use around people and animals. That said, "probiotic" on a label isn't a regulated guarantee, so it's worth knowing what to look for — ingredient transparency, certifications where they exist, and how the product is meant to be used.

We went through the evidence and the caveats here: [Are probiotic cleaning products safe around pets?](/science/are-probiotic-cleaning-products-safe-around-pets).

One practical note that belongs in every home regardless of cleaner choice: never mix a probiotic cleaner with a disinfectant on the same surface at the same time — the disinfectant knocks out the beneficial bacteria you just applied.
